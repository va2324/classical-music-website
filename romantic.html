The Romantic Era

1820-1900

The Romantic Era arrived in the midst of a turbulent time in world history. At the end of the 1700s, monarchies and aristocrats around the world were losing their power over the people they ruled over. The people sought greater rights and freedoms regarding their own lives, and thus, the world saw the spread of political and economic freedoms in the form of democracy and the rising middle class. This spirit affected the music of the time as well, as composers started gaining more freedom in their creativity. The rise of a prosperous middle class meant that more people could afford leisure activities, such as attending musical concerts. This allowed musicians to make a decent living as concert performers and composers for the public, and so they were no longer dependent on rich nobles and monarchs for support.

During this time, composers began to break the rules of the Classical Era and create more dramatic and personal music inspired by their own ideas and emotions. The Romantic Era is named for how composers would romanticize aspects of life in their music. Many composers drew inspiration from poetry, art, folk legends, nature, and even their own imaginations. Since many composers were expressing their own feelings, Romantic music varies greatly. Some pieces are short, simple, and played by one instrument while others are long complex, and played by a huge orchestra. To express emotions, composers experimented with large dynamic ranges (from incredibly soft to incredibly loud) and unusual harmonies. They also used dissonance (notes that do not sound right together) to create scary sounds and key changes to illustrate changes in mood. Some of the most famous Romantic composers are Franz Lizst, Frederic Chopin, and Johannes Brahms.
